Aqueous Extraction : Hydraulic Mining

Hydraulic Mining utilized a high-pressure stream of water is directed against a bank to undercut and cave it. As the material disintegrates, the loosened particles are slurried in water and washed into a sluice

It is generally used for weakly cemented near-surface ore deposits.

It usually used a high-pressure stream of water that is directed against the mineral deposit (normally but not always a placer) to undercut and cave it.

.
×

Explanation

As the material breaks down, the freed particles like minerals, sand, and gravel mix with water and flow into a sluice, which can be a natural channel in the ground or a man-made metal or wooden container. There, they move downhill by gravity and enter a riffle box or a more advanced concentrating device

×

Conditions

Ore Strength Prefer heavy minerals in unconsolidated soil or gravel with minimum boulders
Rock strength Unconsolidated.
Deposit shape Placer type, tabular, bank or bench
Deposit dip Nearly flat (2-6% grade)
Deposit size Small to intermediate with thickness of 15-200 ft (5-60 m).
Ore grade Can be very low
Ore uniformity Fairly uniform
Depth Very shallow, little overburden
×

Advantages

1. Fairly high productivity of 100-300 yd3 (75-230 m3) per employee-shift

2. Low mining cost (relative cost about 5%)

3. Intermediate production rate

4. Low capital cost; simple equipment and cycle.

5. Can automate some operations

6. Can operate a mine with a few workers.

Disadvantages

1. Environmental damage is severe unless elaborate protection is implemented.

2. Extensive water requirements.

3. Limited to unconsolidated deposits that disintegrate under hydraulic attack.

4. Cutting action is difficult to control.
