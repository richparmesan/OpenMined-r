Aqueous Extraction : Dredge Mining

Dredge Mining uses a floating vessel to extract minerals from a placer deposit mechanically or hydraulically. Dredging is the underwater excavation of placer deposits, usually carried out from floating vessel called dredge, which may incorporate processing and waste disposal facilities.

Dredging is the process of underwater mining/ excavation of a placer deposit lying over the floor of natural pond, lake, river, sea, etc. This method often used to bring up underwater mineral deposits.

This method consists of two (2) types.

1. Shallow Water Methods

- This method is often classified by method of excavation and materials transport.
- The two classification of Shallow water Mining methods by Transportation.

1. Mechanical Dredge

- The placer deposits are mechanically excavated and transported.

- Example: Bucketline Dredges exhibit continuous excavation efficiency, mining, processing, and discarding tailings seamlessly. The system lacks storage options, relying on gravity to move the stream. Buckets on a ladder dig the mine face, transporting materials to a hopper that feeds the washing plant, showcasing high excavation capabilities.

2. Hydraulic Dredge (Suction Dredge)

- In this method, water is used as a transportation medium.

- Suction Cutter dredges employ rotating cutting arms within a basket around a suction intake. These arms break up bank material, turning it into a slurry for easy suction into the dredge. This method has demonstrated success in extracting unconsolidated beach sand and offshore placers.

2. Deep sea (Marine Mining) Methods

- Deep-sea mining is the extraction of valuable minerals from the ocean floor at significant depths. Specialized equipment is used to harvest mineral deposits like polymetallic nodules, sulphides, and cobalt-rich crusts. This process occurs in areas with high concentrations of metals such as copper, nickel, cobalt, and rare earth elements. Ongoing research and debate focus on the environmental impact and sustainability of deep-sea mining, raising concerns about potential harm to deep-sea ecosystems and biodiversity.

Cycle Operation

The uninterrupted operation of the dredge reduces disruptions in handling materials. While water aids in digging, the majority of excavation is mechanized. Mineral processing, frequently employing wet gravity separation, takes place on the dredge itself. Waste is deposited directly into the dredge's pond, gradually filling it as the dredge advances. The process of reclamation is smoothly integrated into the dredging procedure.

Conditions

Ore Strength unconsolidated deposit of soil, gravel, or similar material; some boulders permissible, depending on dredge type; prefer valuable mineral to be heavier than waste
Rock strength Unconsolidated.
Deposit shape placer, tabular, bank or bench
Deposit dip preferably flat (maximum of 2% to 6% grade)
Deposit size intermediate to large (thickness 25 to 200 ft, or 8 to 60 m)
Ore grade can be very low
Ore uniformity fairly uniform
Depth very shallow, little overburden
Other moderate quantities of water required (200 to 2000 gal/min, or 13 to 125 1/sec)

Advantages

1. Most productive of all mining methods (250 to 400 yd3, or 190 to 300 m3, of gravel per employee-shift).

2. Lowest mining cost (relative cost < 5%).

3. High production rate (maximum of 9 million yd3 or 7 million m3 a year)

4. Low labor requirements (crew of 2 to 30 people).

5. Good recovery (up to 90%).

6. Continuous operation with no breakage required.

Disadvantages

1. Environmental damage can be severe; environmental protection must be exercised; outlawed in some states.

2. Moderate water requirements (600 to 800 gal/yd3, or 3000 to 4000 1/m3, of material mined).

3. Limited to unconsolidated deposits that disintegrate under hydraulic or mechanical attack.

4. High capital investment for large dredges.

5. Inflexible and unselective; limited to placer-type deposits
