Aqueous Extraction : Borehole Mining

Borehole extraction employs wells to access underground minerals, using water and lixiviant for techniques like melting, leaching, dissolution, and creating mineral slurries for recovery.

General Process:

1. Melting sulfur (e.g., the Frasch process).

2. In situ leaching for uranium, copper, gold, and silver.

3. Dissolving salt, potash, and trona Forming slurries of phosphate, kaolin, oil sands, coal, gilsonite, and uranium (technically feasible but not widely adopted due to economic considerations).

Melting, dissolution, and slurrying are typically done in single wells, each functioning as a production unit.

Conditions

Ore Strength reasonably competent but porous and permeable (alternatives: must dissolve, melt, or slurry in water)
Rock strength surrounding rock must be competent and impervious
Deposit shape any, but prefer tabular deposit with large extent
Deposit dip any, preferably flat or low
Deposit size moderate to large, thickness > 50 ft (15 m)
Ore grade intermediate (sulfur > 5%)
Ore uniformity variable to uniform
Depth ntermediate to high (sulfur generally 200 to 2500 ft or 60 to 750 m; typically less than 10,000 ft or 3000 m for other commodities)
Other moderate to large quantities required
×

Advantages

1. High productivity and moderate production cost.

2. Low capital cost as compared with other methods

3. Low mining cost (average relative cost: 5%).

4. Applicable to deep and low-grade deposits.

5. Reduced development time and cost.

6. Continuous operation.

7. Breakage not required

8. Disturbs less surface area.

Disadvantages

1. Limited to deposits that dissolve, melt, or slurry in water.

2. Moderate water requirements

3. Testing often proves difficult.

4. Frasch process requires significant amounts of energy.

5. Unselective: Control of the extraction zone is difficult.

6. Recovery normally low to moderate

7. Possible groundwater contamination
