Glossary

Aggregates: A mass or body of rock particles, mineral grains, or a mixture of both; any of several hard, inert materials, such as sand, gravel, slag, or crushed stone, mixed with a cement or bituminous material to form concrete, mortar, or plaster, or used alone, as in railroad ballast or graded fill. The term can include rock material used as chemical or metallurgical fluxstone.

Alluvium: Soil or sediment transported and deposited by flowing water.

Area Mining/Strip Mining: Open cut or open-pit surface mining that is carried on the level or along the prevailing topography in which relatively large tracts are disturbed. Usually mined with large shovels or draglines.

Base Failure: Occurs when there is a weak soil strata under the toe and failure plane passes through the base of the slope. Rotational failure can be seen in finite slopes such as earthen dams, embankments, man-made slopes, etc.

Bench Height: The vertical distance between the base of one bench and the base of the overlying bench. The bench height is designed by the mine planner. The main constraint on the height of the bench is usually the limiting thickness that can be handled by the drills and/or the loading equipment.

Bench: A ledge that forms a single level of operation above which mineral or waste materials are mined from the bench face.

Berm Width: The width of the horizontal distance between berm toe of the upper berm and berm crest of the next lower berm; width of the horizontal surface pits, between benches, in which most activities happen in this like drilling, loading and vehicle travel.

Burden: The volume of rock that lies within the zone of influence of a charge of explosive; the volume of rock to be broken by any hole or charge. Distance between blast holes measured perpendicular to the free face.

Crest: The highest point on the bench.

Dragline: An excavating machine that uses a bucket operated and suspended by cables and a boom. The bucket is dragged back toward the machine along the surface and usually operates from the highwall or above the area in which soil or spoil is being removed.

Dredging: Frequently used in mineral sand mining to recover the ore from the mine face as a water slurry by suction. The dredge houses a powerful pump and is floated on pontoons in the dredge pond. In a suction cutter dredge, ore recovery is assisted by a revolving open basket (cutter) mounted over the suction inlet. In hard ground, the cutter can be replaced by a rotating underwater bucket wheel.

Drop Cut: The initial cut made in the floor of an open-pit mine or quarry for the purpose of developing a bench at a level below the floor.

Exploration: Involves prospecting, sampling, mapping, diamond drilling, and other activities in the search for valuable mineral deposits.

Face: Is the vertical to intermediate dipping wall created in rock by excavation actions.

Flotation: Is a milling process in which valuable mineral particles are induced to attach to bubbles and float, while others sink. Froth flotation is a technique employed to separate materials that attract water from those that repel it. In mining, this method is utilized to separate valuable minerals or metals from waste material, causing them to accumulate on the surface in a 'froth' layer.

Hydraulic Mining or Hydraulicking: A form of mining that uses high-pressure jets of water to dislodge rock material or move sediment. In the placer mining of gold or tin, the resulting water sediment slurry is directed through sluice boxes to remove the gold.

Iron Ore: Refers to rocks and minerals that contain metallic iron and can be economically extracted.

Leachable: Refers to the capability of being extracted by chemical solvents.

Leaching: A chemical process for extracting valuable minerals from ore. It also refers to the natural process in which groundwater dissolves minerals, resulting in the rock having a reduced proportion of some minerals compared to its original composition.

Mine Waste: Broad group of waste materials resulting from the extraction of metals and non-metals. The wastes include solid, as well as liquid waste, and can be inert or can contain hazardous constituents. In general, mine waste consists of high volume, low toxicity wastes.

Mining: Is the extraction of valuable minerals or other geological materials from the Earth, usually from an ore body, lode, vein, seam, reef or placer deposit; the process or industry of obtaining coal or other minerals from a mine.

Nugget: A small mass of precious metal that is naturally found in a free state.

Ore: Rock containing minerals that have economic value.

Overbreak: Rock or earth excavated beyond the planned extent; caving in of material near the edge of an excavation.

Overburden: Material of any nature, consolidated or unconsolidated, that overlies a deposit of useful and minable materials or ores, especially those deposits that are mined from the surface by open cuts or pits. Overburden material; may generally refer to either an area and/or a contour mine.

Overhang: Part of a rock face that exceeds the vertical.

Panning: To wash gravel, sand or crushed rock samples in order to isolate gold or other valuable metals by their higher density.

Pit Floor: Or “quarry floor” means the lower limit of a surface excavation to extract minerals.

Pit Limit: The plan extent of mining after all ore and waste has been taken out. The limits are a function of economics and physical factors such as faulting, lease boundaries and so on. Even if you do not intend to reach the mining limits for 30 years, it is important to know where the limits are.

Placer: A deposit of sand and gravel containing valuable metals such as gold, tin, or diamonds.

Prospecting: Is the first stage of the geological analysis followed by exploration of a territory. It is the search for minerals, fossils, precious metals, or mineral specimens.

Quarry: Any open or surface workings, usually for the extraction of sand and gravel, building stone, slate, or limestone.

Safety Berms: A berm refers to dirt and rock piled alongside a haulage road or along the edge of a dump point and is intended as a safety measure. They are used around dump points and along haul roads to protect heavy mining vehicles from running over an edge.

Slope Angle: The angle measured from the bottom bench toe to the top bench crest. It is the angle at which the wall of an open pit stands and it is determined by rock strength, geologic structures, and water conditions.

Slope Failure: Is a phenomenon that a slope collapses abruptly due to weakened self-retain ability of the earth under the influence of rainfall or an earthquake.

Sluice Box: An elongate wooden or metal trough with riffles, over which alluvial gravel is washed to recover gold.

Sluicing: Jets of water are being directed onto a gravel slope in a mining process known as sluicing.

Stripping Ratio: The unit amount of overburden that must be removed to gain access to a similar unit amount of coal or mineral material.

Solution Mining: Is applied to recover deep deposits that would be economically unfeasible using underground methods, provided the ore can be readily dissolved by a solvent.

Tailings: Denote the material rejected from a mill after extracting the majority of valuable minerals.

Tailings Pond: A shallow basin designed to contain tailings. Its primary purpose is to allow sufficient time for heavy metals to settle or for cyanide to be neutralized before discharging water into the local watershed.

Toe: The lowest point on the bench; a remnant of rock left unbroken at the floor of the quarry face by an unsatisfactory blast.
